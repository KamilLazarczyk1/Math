Procedures — Foolproof Step-by-Step Methods

One numbered recipe per task type. Follow the steps in order and you cannot leave anything out or pick the wrong method. Each recipe lists the formulas in the simplest form, with a key explaining every symbol.

Notation used everywhere on this page. Variables are $x_1,x_2,\dots$ (like the worksheets). $\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}$ means "differentiate $f$ with respect to $x_i$, treating every other letter as a constant." A dot $\cdot$ between vectors is the dot product: $(a_1,a_2)\cdot(b_1,b_2)=a_1b_1+a_2b_2$.

1. ODE — general solution of $a\,y''+b\,y'+c\,y=g(t)$

Use when: you see a second-order equation with constant numbers in front of $y''$, $y'$, $y$.
Make $y''$ stand alone

Divide the whole equation by the number in front of $y''$ so it reads $\,y''+p\,y'+q\,y=g(t)$. Note what $g(t)$ (the right side) is — you'll need it.

Solve the homogeneous part (the $y_h$ with the two constants)

Write the characteristic equation and find its roots $r$:

$$ r^2 + p\,r + q = 0 \qquad\Rightarrow\qquad r=\frac{-p\pm\sqrt{p^2-4q}}{2}. $$

Then pick the matching shape:

Two different real roots $r_1\neq r_2$:   $y_h=c_1e^{r_1 t}+c_2e^{r_2 t}$.
One repeated root $r$:   $y_h=(c_1+c_2 t)e^{r t}$.
Complex roots $r=\alpha\pm\beta i$:   $y_h=e^{\alpha t}\big(c_1\cos\beta t+c_2\sin\beta t\big)$.
Choose the method for a particular solution $y_p$
Decide
  • If $g(t)$ is a polynomial, an $e^{at}$, a $\cos/\sin$, or any product/sum of these → Step 4 (undetermined coefficients).
  • If $g(t)$ is anything else ($\tan t$, $\dfrac{1}{e^t+1}$, $\dfrac{e^t}{t}$, $\ln$, …) → Step 5 (variation of parameters).
Undetermined coefficients
(a) Guess the shape. For each piece of $g(t)$ write a guess with unknown letters:
$e^{at}\to A\,e^{at}$;   degree-$n$ polynomial $\to A_nt^n+\dots+A_1t+A_0$ (the full polynomial);   $\cos bt$ or $\sin bt\to A\cos bt+B\sin bt$ (always both);   a product $\to$ the product of the guesses.
(b) Resonance fix. If your guess (ignoring the letters) already appears in $y_h$, multiply that guess by $t$. If it still appears (a repeated root), multiply by $t^2$.
(c) Plug in. Substitute the guess into $y''+p\,y'+q\,y$ and expand.
(d) Match. Set the coefficient of each function (each power of $t$, each $\cos$, $\sin$, $e^{at}$) equal on both sides. Solve the resulting linear system for the unknown letters.
Variation of parameters (works for any $g$)
(a) Take the two independent homogeneous pieces $y_1,y_2$ from Step 2 (e.g. $y_1=e^{r_1t},y_2=e^{r_2t}$, or $\cos\beta t,\sin\beta t$, …).
(b) Compute the Wronskian $\;W=y_1y_2'-y_1'y_2$.
(c) Integrate (drop the $+C$): $$ u_1=\int\frac{-\,y_2\,g}{W}\,dt,\qquad u_2=\int\frac{y_1\,g}{W}\,dt. $$ Here $g$ is the right side after Step 1 (with $y''$ alone).
(d) $\;y_p=u_1y_1+u_2y_2$. If any term is just a copy of $y_1$ or $y_2$, delete it (it merges into $c_1,c_2$).
Write the general solution
$$ y=y_h+y_p. $$
If initial conditions $y(0),y'(0)$ are given
(a) Differentiate your general solution to get $y'(t)$.
(b) Put $t=0$ into $y$ and into $y'$ → two equations (use $e^0=1,\cos0=1,\sin0=0$).
(c) Solve the two equations for $c_1,c_2$ and write the final answer.
Final check

Substitute your $y_p$ into the left side $y''+p\,y'+q\,y$; you must get exactly $g(t)$.

Key: $y_h$ = homogeneous solution (carries $c_1,c_2$); $y_p$ = one particular solution; $r$ = root of the characteristic equation; $W$ = Wronskian; $g(t)$ = right-hand side.

2. Gradient & directional derivative

Use when: asked for $\nabla f$, the gradient, or the rate of change of $f$ in a direction $v$.
Gradient = all first partials, listed as a vector
$$ \nabla f=\Big(\tfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_1},\ \tfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_2},\ \dots,\ \tfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}\Big). $$

For each $\tfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}$: differentiate $f$ in $x_i$, holding all other letters constant.

If a point $x_0$ is given, plug it in

Substitute the coordinates of $x_0$ into each partial to get the gradient vector $\nabla f(x_0)$ (a list of numbers).

Directional derivative along a vector $v$
$$ \frac{\partial f}{\partial v}(x_0)=\nabla f(x_0)\cdot v=\tfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}v_1+\tfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_2}v_2+\dots $$
Important
  • This course uses $v$ exactly as given — do not normalize it.
  • Only if a problem explicitly says "unit direction" or "per unit length": divide the answer by $|v|=\sqrt{v_1^2+v_2^2+\dots}$.
Key: $\nabla f$ = gradient (vector of slopes, points uphill); $v=(v_1,v_2,\dots)$ = direction vector; $\cdot$ = dot product.

3. Check differentiability at a point $x_0$

Use when: asked "is $f$ differentiable at $x_0$?" (often $x_0=(0,0)$, often $f$ defined piecewise). Do the cheap tests first.
Continuity test (cheapest disqualifier)

Check whether $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to x_0}f(x)=f(x_0)$.

Fast disproof: try two simple paths into $x_0$ (e.g. along $x_2=0$, then along $x_2=x_1$). If two paths give different limits, the limit doesn't exist → $f$ is not continuous → not differentiable. STOP.
To confirm continuity: bound it — if $|f(x)-f(x_0)|\le(\text{something that}\to0)$, it is continuous; continue.
Partial derivatives at $x_0$ (from the definition)

When $f$ is defined specially at $x_0$, compute the partials there with the limit definition:

$$ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}(x_0)=\lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(x_0+h\,\mathbf e_1)-f(x_0)}{h},\quad \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2}(x_0)=\lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(x_0+h\,\mathbf e_2)-f(x_0)}{h}. $$

($\mathbf e_1$ = step in the $x_1$-direction only, $\mathbf e_2$ = step in $x_2$ only.) If a partial doesn't exist → not differentiable. STOP.

The definition test (the decisive one)

$f$ is differentiable at $x_0$ exactly when this limit is $0$:

$$ \lim_{x\to x_0}\frac{\,f(x)-f(x_0)-\nabla f(x_0)\cdot(x-x_0)\,}{|x-x_0|}=0. $$
Write $x-x_0$ as the small displacement, plug in the partials from Step 2.
If the limit $=0$ (e.g. you can bound the fraction by something $\to0$) → differentiable. If it is nonzero on some path → not differentiable.
Shortcut If both partials exist and are continuous functions near $x_0$, then $f$ is automatically differentiable — you may skip Step 3. (But Step 3 is the definition and never fails.)
Key: $|x-x_0|=\sqrt{(x_1-x_{01})^2+(x_2-x_{02})^2}$ = distance to $x_0$;   $\nabla f(x_0)\cdot(x-x_0)$ = the tangent-plane (linear) approximation.   Logic chain: differentiable $\Rightarrow$ continuous, but "partials exist" does not give differentiable.

4. Local extrema with the Hessian

Use when: asked to find local maxima/minima of a scalar function $f(x_1,\dots,x_n)$.
Find the critical points

Set every first partial to zero and solve the system:

$$ \nabla f=0:\qquad \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}=0,\ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2}=0,\ \dots,\ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}=0. $$

Every solution is a candidate point.

Build the Hessian (matrix of second partials)
$$ H=\Big[\tfrac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_i\,\partial x_j}\Big]\quad\Big(\text{2 vars: }H=\begin{bmatrix}f_{x_1x_1}&f_{x_1x_2}\\ f_{x_1x_2}&f_{x_2x_2}\end{bmatrix}\Big). $$

$f_{x_ix_j}$ means differentiate $f$ by $x_i$, then by $x_j$. $H$ is symmetric.

At each critical point, plug in numbers and classify
Option A — eigenvalues Solve $\det(H-\lambda I)=0$ for the eigenvalues $\lambda$:
  • all $\lambda\gt0$ → local minimum
  • all $\lambda\lt0$ → local maximum
  • both signs appear → saddle (no extremum)
  • some $\lambda=0$, rest one sign → inconclusive
Option B — 2×2 shortcut Let $D=\det H=f_{x_1x_1}f_{x_2x_2}-(f_{x_1x_2})^2$:
  • $D\gt0$ and $f_{x_1x_1}\gt0$ → minimum
  • $D\gt0$ and $f_{x_1x_1}\lt0$ → maximum
  • $D\lt0$ → saddle
  • $D=0$ → inconclusive
Report values

For each max/min, compute $f$ at that point if the value is asked for.

Key: critical point = where the gradient is zero; $H$ = Hessian; $\lambda$ = eigenvalue; $I$ = identity matrix; $\det$ = determinant.

5. Critical points with the Jacobian

Use when: $f=(f_1,\dots,f_n)$ is a vector field (same number of outputs as inputs) and you must find its critical points (equilibria).
Set the whole output to zero

A critical point is where every component is zero:

$$ f(x_0)=0:\qquad f_1=0,\ f_2=0,\ \dots,\ f_n=0. $$

Solve this system; each solution is a critical point.

Build the Jacobian (for the stability question next)
$$ J_f=\Big[\tfrac{\partial f_i}{\partial x_j}\Big]\quad\Big(\text{2 comps: }J_f=\begin{bmatrix}\partial f_1/\partial x_1 & \partial f_1/\partial x_2\\ \partial f_2/\partial x_1 & \partial f_2/\partial x_2\end{bmatrix}\Big). $$

Row $i$ = the partials of $f_i$; column $j$ = derivative with respect to $x_j$.

Key: $f_i$ = the $i$-th component (output) of $f$; $J_f$ = Jacobian (matrix of first partials). To then judge stability, go to recipe 6.

6. Is a critical point asymptotically stable?

Use when: you have a critical point $x_0$ of $f$ and must say whether it is asymptotically stable or not.
Jacobian at the point → a number matrix

Build $J_f$ (recipe 5, Step 2) and substitute the coordinates of $x_0$. Now it is a plain matrix of numbers.

Find its eigenvalues

Solve $\det\!\big(J_f(x_0)-\lambda I\big)=0$. For a $2\times2$ matrix $\begin{bmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{bmatrix}$ this is

$$ \lambda^2-(\underbrace{a+d}_{\text{trace}})\,\lambda+(\underbrace{ad-bc}_{\det})=0 \;\Rightarrow\; \lambda=\frac{(a+d)\pm\sqrt{(a+d)^2-4(ad-bc)}}{2}. $$
Read off the real parts
Verdict
  • All eigenvalues have $\operatorname{Re}(\lambda)\lt0$ → asymptotically stable.
  • At least one eigenvalue has $\operatorname{Re}(\lambda)\gt0$ → not stable.
  • Largest real part $=0$ (none positive) → this test is inconclusive.

If $\lambda=\alpha\pm\beta i$ is complex, its real part is $\operatorname{Re}(\lambda)=\alpha$ (the part without $i$).

Key: trace $=a+d$ = sum of diagonal entries; $\det=ad-bc$; $\operatorname{Re}(\lambda)$ = real part of the eigenvalue. Do this once per critical point.

7. Chain rule / implicit differentiation (like WS9 Ex 3)

Use when: you differentiate something in $t$ where the variables themselves depend on $t$.

Case A — a function $V$ evaluated along a moving point $x(t)$

Use the chain-rule formula
$$ \frac{d}{dt}V\big(x(t)\big)=\nabla V\cdot x'(t)=\frac{\partial V}{\partial x_1}\,x_1'(t)+\frac{\partial V}{\partial x_2}\,x_2'(t)+\dots $$
Fill in the three ingredients

(a) compute $\nabla V$;  (b) write the given $x_1'(t),x_2'(t),\dots$;  (c) form the dot product;  (d) substitute the given formulas and simplify.

Case B — an implicit relation $F\big(t,x(t)\big)=\text{constant}$

Differentiate both sides in $t$

Every time you differentiate $x$, write $x'$ (because $x$ depends on $t$). Use the product rule on mixed terms like $t\,x$. The right side (a constant) gives $0$.

Building blocks:   $\dfrac{d}{dt}\big[x(t)^2\big]=2x\,x'$,   $\dfrac{d}{dt}\big[t\,x(t)\big]=x+t\,x'$,   $\dfrac{d}{dt}\big[t^2\big]=2t$,   $\dfrac{d}{dt}[\text{const}]=0$.
Collect terms

Group the $x'$ terms together and the rest together — that is the required equation.

8. Volume / double integral over a region $\Omega$

Use when: asked for $\iint_\Omega f\,dx$ over a 2-D region.
Describe the region by slices

For each height $x_2$ from $c$ to $d$, find where $x_1$ enters and leaves: from a left edge $\ell(x_2)$ to a right edge $r(x_2)$. (To get the edges, write each slanted side as a line and solve for $x_1$.)

Write the iterated integral
$$ \iint_\Omega f\,dx=\int_{c}^{d}\!\left(\int_{\ell(x_2)}^{r(x_2)} f(x_1,x_2)\,dx_1\right)dx_2. $$
Inner integral first, then outer

Integrate over $x_1$ treating $x_2$ as a constant; put in the limits. Then integrate the result over $x_2$.

Time-saver: over a symmetric interval $[-a,a]$, an odd function integrates to $0$; an even function gives $2\!\int_0^a$.
If a change of variables $\Phi$ is given ($\Phi$ maps a simpler region $\Omega'$ onto $\Omega$): $$ \iint_\Omega f(x)\,dx=\iint_{\Omega'} f\big(\Phi(u)\big)\,\big|\det J_\Phi(u)\big|\,du. $$ Steps: build $J_\Phi$ (its Jacobian matrix), take $\det$, take absolute value, multiply by $f(\Phi(u))$, integrate over the simpler region $\Omega'$.
Key: $\Omega$ = region; $\ell,r$ = left/right boundaries of a horizontal slice; $|\det J_\Phi|$ = how much the map $\Phi$ stretches area.

9. Surface integral of $f$ over a surface $S$ in 3-D

Use when: asked for $\iint_S f\,d\sigma$ over a curved surface (a parametrization is usually given or hinted).
Get a parametrization

Write the surface as $\sigma(u_1,u_2)=(x_1,x_2,x_3)$, with $(u_1,u_2)$ ranging over a flat region $D$. (Example: $\sigma(u_1,u_2)=(u_1,u_2,u_1u_2)$ for a graph $x_3=x_1x_2$.)

Two tangent vectors
$$ \sigma_{u_1}=\frac{\partial\sigma}{\partial u_1},\qquad \sigma_{u_2}=\frac{\partial\sigma}{\partial u_2}\quad(\text{each a 3-D vector}). $$
Cross product, then its length (the area element)
$$ N=\sigma_{u_1}\times\sigma_{u_2},\qquad |N|=\sqrt{N_1^2+N_2^2+N_3^2}. $$

Cross product of $a=(a_1,a_2,a_3)$ and $b=(b_1,b_2,b_3)$:

$$ a\times b=(a_2b_3-a_3b_2,\ \ a_3b_1-a_1b_3,\ \ a_1b_2-a_2b_1). $$
Put the surface into $f$

Replace $x_1,x_2,x_3$ in $f$ by the components of $\sigma$ to get $f\big(\sigma(u_1,u_2)\big)$. (Often a square root cancels here — watch for it.)

Integrate over the flat region $D$
$$ \iint_S f\,d\sigma=\iint_D f\big(\sigma(u_1,u_2)\big)\,|N|\,du_1\,du_2. $$

Set the limits of $D$ and compute (inner integral first, then outer).

Key: $\sigma$ = parametrization (surface written with two parameters); $\sigma_{u_1},\sigma_{u_2}$ = tangent vectors; $\times$ = cross product; $|N|$ = length of the normal = area-stretch factor; $d\sigma=|N|\,du_1\,du_2$ = surface area element.
