Multiple & Surface Integrals

Integrating over a 2D region by slicing, the change-of-variables formula with the Jacobian determinant, and integrating a function over a curved surface in $\mathbb{R}^3$ via a parametrization.

Start here — the whole idea in plain words

An integral just means "add up a huge number of tiny pieces." A normal integral adds pieces along a line; here we add pieces over a flat region, and then over a curved surface.

Double integral = weighing a sheet.

Picture this: a metal sheet whose thickness varies from place to place. To get its total weight, chop it into tiny squares, weigh each tiny square, and add them all up. "Slicing" just means: add up along one horizontal strip first, then add up all the strips.

Change of variables = redraw the map to make the shape simple. The region here is an awkward trapezoid. We switch to friendlier coordinates that turn it into a tidy rectangle. But stretching a map changes areas, so we multiply by a correction factor — the Jacobian determinant.

Picture this: it's an exchange rate for area — it converts "one unit of area on the new map" into "how much real area that actually covers." It's the 2-D version of the $\frac{dx}{du}$ factor you already use in ordinary substitution.

Surface integral = how much paint covers a curved hood.

Picture this: you describe a curved car hood with a flat sheet of graph paper (a "parametrization"). A small square on the flat paper maps to a bigger, tilted patch on the real curved hood. The length of the cross product $|\sigma_{u_1}\times\sigma_{u_2}|$ is exactly that "how much bigger" stretch factor. In Exercise 2 this stretch factor neatly cancels the square root hiding in $f$, which is why a scary-looking integral collapses into the easy $\iint u_1u_2$.

The tools

Double integral over a region (slicing)

To integrate over a non-rectangular region $\Omega$, slice it. If for each height $x_2\in[c,d]$ the region runs horizontally from $x_1=\ell(x_2)$ to $x_1=r(x_2)$, then

$$ \iint_\Omega f(x_1,x_2)\,dx = \int_c^d\!\!\left(\int_{\ell(x_2)}^{r(x_2)} f(x_1,x_2)\,dx_1\right)dx_2. $$

You do the inside integral first (treating $x_2$ as constant), then the outside one.

Change of variables (the Jacobian)

This is the multivariable version of $u$-substitution. If $\Phi$ maps a region $\Omega'$ onto $\Omega$ (one-to-one), then

$$ \iint_\Omega f(x)\,dx = \iint_{\Omega'} f\big(\Phi(u)\big)\,\big|\det J_\Phi(u)\big|\,du. $$

The factor $|\det J_\Phi|$ is the local area-stretching factor of the map $\Phi$ — it plays the role of $\left|\frac{dx}{du}\right|$ from one-variable substitution. Here $J_\Phi$ is the Jacobian matrix of $\Phi$.

Surface integral via a parametrization

Let a surface $S$ be described by $\sigma(u_1,u_2)$ for $(u_1,u_2)$ in a parameter region $D$. The surface area element is the length of the cross product of the two tangent vectors:

$$ d\sigma = \left|\frac{\partial\sigma}{\partial u_1}\times\frac{\partial\sigma}{\partial u_2}\right|\,du_1\,du_2, $$ $$ \iint_S f\,d\sigma = \iint_D f\big(\sigma(u_1,u_2)\big)\,\left|\frac{\partial\sigma}{\partial u_1}\times\frac{\partial\sigma}{\partial u_2}\right|\,du_1\,du_2. $$

The cross product $\sigma_{u_1}\times\sigma_{u_2}$ is the (un-normalized) normal to the surface; its length measures how much area an little $du_1\,du_2$ patch covers on $S$.

Official vs. own. The single-variable integrals (powers of $u$) are on official sheet. The double-integral slicing setup, the change-of-variables / Jacobian formula, the cross-product formula, and the surface-element formula are not on the official sheets bring yourself.

Exercise 1 — Double integral over a quadrilateral

$\Omega=ABCD$ with $A=(-2,0),\ B=(2,0),\ C=(1,1),\ D=(-1,1)$,   $f(x_1,x_2)=x_1^2-2x_2x_1$.

(a) Direct computation

Describe the region. $\Omega$ is a trapezoid. The bottom edge $AB$ sits at $x_2=0$, the top edge $DC$ at $x_2=1$. The slanted sides are lines:

  • Left side $A(-2,0)\to D(-1,1)$: slope $\tfrac{\Delta x_1}{\Delta x_2}=\tfrac{-1-(-2)}{1-0}=1$, so $x_1=-2+x_2$.
  • Right side $B(2,0)\to C(1,1)$: similarly $x_1=2-x_2$.

So for each $x_2\in[0,1]$, $x_1$ runs from $-(2-x_2)$ to $(2-x_2)$ — a symmetric interval about $0$. With $a:=2-x_2$,

$$ \iint_\Omega f\,dx = \int_0^1\!\!\int_{-a}^{a}\big(x_1^2-2x_2 x_1\big)\,dx_1\,dx_2. $$

Inner integral. Over the symmetric interval $[-a,a]$, $x_1^2$ is even and $x_1$ is odd, so

$$ \int_{-a}^{a}x_1^2\,dx_1=\frac{2a^3}{3},\qquad \int_{-a}^{a}(-2x_2 x_1)\,dx_1=0. $$
spotting symmetry saves work. The slice runs from $-a$ to $+a$ (symmetric about $0$). The term with a lone $x_1$ is "odd" — its left half and right half are mirror images with opposite signs, so they perfectly cancel and the integral is $0$ without computing anything.
$$ \Rightarrow\quad \int_{-a}^{a}f\,dx_1=\frac{2}{3}(2-x_2)^3. $$

Outer integral. Substitute $u=2-x_2$ ($du=-dx_2$; $x_2{=}0\!\to\!u{=}2$, $x_2{=}1\!\to\!u{=}1$):

$$ \int_0^1\frac{2}{3}(2-x_2)^3\,dx_2=\frac{2}{3}\int_1^2 u^3\,du=\frac{2}{3}\cdot\frac{u^4}{4}\Big|_1^2=\frac{2}{3}\cdot\frac{16-1}{4}=\frac{2}{3}\cdot\frac{15}{4}=\frac{5}{2}. $$
$\displaystyle\iint_\Omega f\,dx=\frac{5}{2}$ — matches the given answer.

(b) Confirm via the change-of-variables formula

The map is $\Phi(x_1,x_2)=\big(x_1(2-x_2),\,x_2\big)$ on $[-1,1]\times[0,1]$. (Check it really covers $\Omega$: as $x_1$ runs over $[-1,1]$, the first coordinate $x_1(2-x_2)$ sweeps $[-(2-x_2),\,2-x_2]$, exactly the horizontal slice found above.)

Jacobian matrix.

$$ J_\Phi(x)=\begin{pmatrix}\partial_{x_1}[x_1(2-x_2)] & \partial_{x_2}[x_1(2-x_2)]\\[2pt] \partial_{x_1}[x_2] & \partial_{x_2}[x_2]\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}2-x_2 & -x_1\\[2pt] 0 & 1\end{pmatrix}, $$ $$ \det J_\Phi=(2-x_2)(1)-(-x_1)(0)=2-x_2\;\gt0\ \text{ (since } x_2\le1), \quad\text{so } |\det J_\Phi|=2-x_2. $$
there's a $0$ in the bottom-left corner, so this matrix is "triangular" and its determinant is just the product of the diagonal, $(2-x_2)\cdot1$. That number is the area exchange rate: a tiny tile in the $(x_1,x_2)$ rectangle covers $(2-x_2)$ times as much area after the map $\Phi$ stretches it.

Transform the integrand. With $\Phi_1=x_1(2-x_2)$ and $\Phi_2=x_2$,

$$ f(\Phi(x))=\Phi_1^2-2\Phi_2\Phi_1=x_1^2(2-x_2)^2-2x_2 x_1(2-x_2). $$

Multiply by $|\det J_\Phi|=(2-x_2)$. Each term picks up one more factor of $(2-x_2)$:

$$ f(\Phi(x))\,|\det J_\Phi| = x_1^2(2-x_2)^2\cdot(2-x_2) - 2x_2 x_1(2-x_2)\cdot(2-x_2) = x_1^2(2-x_2)^3 - 2x_2 x_1(2-x_2)^2. $$

Integrate over the rectangle, doing the $x_1$-integral first. Because $\int_{-1}^1 x_1^2\,dx_1=\tfrac23$ and $\int_{-1}^1 x_1\,dx_1=0$, the second term vanishes:

$$ \int_{-1}^{1}\!\Big[x_1^2(2-x_2)^3-2x_2 x_1(2-x_2)^2\Big]dx_1=(2-x_2)^3\!\int_{-1}^1 x_1^2\,dx_1 - 2x_2(2-x_2)^2\!\int_{-1}^1 x_1\,dx_1=\frac{2}{3}(2-x_2)^3. $$

This is the very same inner result as in part (a), so the outer integral again gives

$\displaystyle\int_{[-1,1]\times[0,1]} f(\Phi(x))\,|\det J_\Phi(x)|\,dx=\frac{5}{2}=\iint_\Omega f\,dx.$ The change-of-variables formula checks out.

Exercise 2 — Surface integral in $\mathbb{R}^3$

$S=\{(x_1,x_2,x_3): 0\le x_1\le1,\ 0\le x_2\le 1-x_1,\ x_3=x_1x_2\}$,   $f(x)=\dfrac{x_3}{\sqrt{1+x_1^2+x_2^2}}$.

Use the suggested parametrization $\sigma(u_1,u_2)=(u_1,\,u_2,\,u_1u_2)$ over the triangle $D:\ 0\le u_1\le1,\ 0\le u_2\le 1-u_1$.

Step 1 — tangent vectors and the surface element

$$ \frac{\partial\sigma}{\partial u_1}=(1,\,0,\,u_2),\qquad \frac{\partial\sigma}{\partial u_2}=(0,\,1,\,u_1). $$

Cross product (expand the determinant):

$$ \frac{\partial\sigma}{\partial u_1}\times\frac{\partial\sigma}{\partial u_2}=\begin{vmatrix}\mathbf i & \mathbf j & \mathbf k\\ 1 & 0 & u_2\\ 0 & 1 & u_1\end{vmatrix}=\big(0\cdot u_1-u_2\cdot1,\ \,-(1\cdot u_1-u_2\cdot0),\ \,1\cdot1-0\big)=(-u_2,\,-u_1,\,1). $$

Its length:

$$ \left|\frac{\partial\sigma}{\partial u_1}\times\frac{\partial\sigma}{\partial u_2}\right|=\sqrt{u_2^2+u_1^2+1}=\sqrt{1+u_1^2+u_2^2}. $$

Step 2 — the integrand simplifies

On the surface, $x_1=u_1,\ x_2=u_2,\ x_3=u_1u_2$, so

$$ f(\sigma(u))=\frac{u_1u_2}{\sqrt{1+u_1^2+u_2^2}}. $$

Multiplying by the surface element, the square roots cancel exactly:

$$ f\,d\sigma = \frac{u_1u_2}{\sqrt{1+u_1^2+u_2^2}}\cdot\sqrt{1+u_1^2+u_2^2}\;du_1\,du_2 = u_1u_2\;du_1\,du_2. $$
this is the whole point of the problem. The ugly $\sqrt{1+u_1^2+u_2^2}$ that the surface-stretch factor produces is the exact same square root sitting in the bottom of $f$, so they cancel. A frightening surface integral quietly becomes "integrate $u_1u_2$ over a triangle."

Step 3 — integrate over the triangle

$$ \iint_S f\,d\sigma=\int_0^1\!\!\int_0^{1-u_1} u_1u_2\,du_2\,du_1 = \int_0^1 u_1\cdot\frac{(1-u_1)^2}{2}\,du_1=\frac12\int_0^1 u_1(1-u_1)^2\,du_1. $$

Expand $u_1(1-u_1)^2=u_1-2u_1^2+u_1^3$ and integrate:

$$ \int_0^1\big(u_1-2u_1^2+u_1^3\big)\,du_1=\frac12-\frac23+\frac14=\frac{6-8+3}{12}=\frac{1}{12}. $$ $$ \Rightarrow\quad \iint_S f\,d\sigma=\frac12\cdot\frac{1}{12}=\frac{1}{24}. $$
$\displaystyle\iint_S f\,d\sigma=\frac{1}{24}$ — matches the given answer.
