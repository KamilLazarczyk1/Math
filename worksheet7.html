Resonance & Variation of Parameters

More undetermined coefficients ŌĆö now with resonance (the $t^s$ rule in action) ŌĆö and the fully general variation of parameters method for forcing terms like $\tan t$, $e^t/t$ and $1/(e^t+1)$ that no simple guess can handle.

Start here ŌĆö the whole idea in plain words

This sheet is the same ODE game as Worksheet 6, with two new wrinkles.

Wrinkle 1 ŌĆö resonance (Exercise 1 & parts of 2): when the thing pushing the system is something the system already does naturally, the simple guess fails, and you patch it by multiplying by $t$ (or $t^2$). Same swing-pushed-at-its-own-rhythm idea as before.

Wrinkle 2 ŌĆö variation of parameters (the new tool): sometimes the push is something ugly like $\tan t$ or $\dfrac{1}{e^t+1}$ ŌĆö there's no neat shape to guess. So we stop guessing and build the answer out of the two natural building-block solutions we already found, but we let the "amounts" of each block change over time instead of being fixed constants.

Picture this: you're building a ramp from two LEGO planks. Normally you'd use a fixed number of each. Here you let the amount of each plank vary as you move along, so together the two planks can trace any curve ŌĆö even a weird one. Two simple recipes (the $u_1',u_2'$ formulas) tell you exactly how much of each plank to add at every step.

The Wronskian $W$ is just a bookkeeping number that checks your two planks are genuinely different shapes (if they weren't, you couldn't build anything new), and it shows up as the denominator. The price for this "works on anything" method is that you have to do two integrals ŌĆö which is where all the real work is in parts (d), (e), (f).

The homogeneous-solution and undetermined-coefficients machinery is explained in detail on the Worksheet 6 theory section. Here we add the one new tool.

Variation of parameters

When do we need it?

Undetermined coefficients only works when $g(t)$ is a polynomial, exponential, sine/cosine, or a product of those ŌĆö because only then do we know what shape to guess. For $g(t)=\tan t$, $\dfrac{e^t}{t}$, or $\dfrac{1}{e^t+1}$ there is no finite guess. Variation of parameters always works (for any continuous $g$), at the cost of computing two integrals.

The formula

First put the equation in standard form with leading coefficient $1$:

$$ y'' + p(t)\,y' + q(t)\,y = g(t). $$

Let $y_1,y_2$ be the two independent homogeneous solutions. Their Wronskian is

$$ W(t) = \begin{vmatrix} y_1 & y_2 \\ y_1' & y_2' \end{vmatrix} = y_1 y_2' - y_1' y_2. $$

Then a particular solution is $y_p = u_1 y_1 + u_2 y_2$, where

$$ u_1' = -\,\frac{y_2\,g}{W}, \qquad u_2' = \frac{y_1\,g}{W}, \qquad\text{so}\qquad u_1=\int -\frac{y_2 g}{W}\,dt,\quad u_2=\int \frac{y_1 g}{W}\,dt. $$

Equivalently, in one line:

$$ y_p(t) = -\,y_1(t)\!\int \frac{y_2(t)\,g(t)}{W(t)}\,dt \;+\; y_2(t)\!\int \frac{y_1(t)\,g(t)}{W(t)}\,dt. $$

Any constant of integration just reproduces a homogeneous solution, so we drop them and fold everything into $c_1,c_2$ at the end.

Exercise 1 ŌĆö Guesses (watch for resonance)

All three of these have the forcing term clashing with a homogeneous solution, so the $t^s$ modification rule kicks in.

a)   $y'' - y = 4e^{t} + 5e^{-t}$

Roots. $r^2-1=0 \Rightarrow r=\pm 1$.

Piece $4e^{t}$: wants $r=1$, a simple root ($s=1$) $\Rightarrow A\,t\,e^{t}$.

Piece $5e^{-t}$: wants $r=-1$, a simple root ($s=1$) $\Rightarrow B\,t\,e^{-t}$.

Guess: $\quad y_p = A\,t\,e^{t} + B\,t\,e^{-t}.$

b)   $y'' + 4y = 4\cos(2t)$

Roots. $r^2+4=0 \Rightarrow r=\pm 2i$.

Forcing $4\cos 2t$ wants $r=\pm 2i$, which are the roots ($s=1$). Multiply the usual $\cos/\sin$ guess by $t$:

Guess: $\quad y_p = t\big(A\cos 2t + B\sin 2t\big).$

c)   $y'' - 2y' + y = 3e^{t}$

Roots. $r^2-2r+1=(r-1)^2=0 \Rightarrow r=1$ (double).

Forcing $3e^{t}$ wants $r=1$, a double root ($s=2$). Multiply $A e^t$ by $t^2$:

Guess: $\quad y_p = A\,t^{2}e^{t}.$

Exercise 2 ŌĆö General solutions

Parts (a)ŌĆō(c) use undetermined coefficients (with resonance in a, b); parts (d)ŌĆō(f) require variation of parameters.

2a)   $y'' - y = 11e^{t}$

Homogeneous. $r=\pm1$, so $y_h=c_1 e^{-t}+c_2 e^{t}$.

Resonance. $e^t$ matches the simple root $r=1$, so guess $y_p = A\,t\,e^{t}$. Then

$$ y_p' = A e^{t}(1+t),\qquad y_p'' = A e^{t}(2+t). $$ $$ y_p''-y_p = A e^{t}(2+t) - A t e^{t} = 2A e^{t} \stackrel{!}{=} 11e^{t}\ \Rightarrow\ A=\tfrac{11}{2}. $$
Answer (matches key): $\quad y = c_1 e^{-t} + c_2 e^{t} + \dfrac{11}{2}\,t e^{t}.$

2b)   $y'' + 4y = 2\sin(2t)$

Homogeneous. $r=\pm 2i$, so $y_h=c_1\cos 2t+c_2\sin 2t$.

Resonance. Guess $y_p = t(A\cos 2t + B\sin 2t)$. Differentiating twice and simplifying,

$$ y_p'' + 4y_p = 4B\cos 2t - 4A\sin 2t. $$

Match $2\sin 2t$ (i.e. $0\cdot\cos 2t + 2\sin 2t$): $4B=0\Rightarrow B=0$ and $-4A=2\Rightarrow A=-\tfrac12$.

Answer (matches key): $\quad y = c_1\cos 2t + c_2\sin 2t - \dfrac{1}{2}\,t\cos 2t.$

2c)   $y'' + 5y' + 4y = t\,e^{t}$

Homogeneous. $r^2+5r+4=(r+1)(r+4)=0\Rightarrow r=-1,-4$, so $y_h=c_1 e^{-4t}+c_2 e^{-t}$.

Guess. $1$ is not a root, so $y_p=(At+B)e^{t}$, with $y_p'=e^{t}(At+A+B)$, $y_p''=e^{t}(At+2A+B)$.

Substitute.

$$ y_p''+5y_p'+4y_p = e^{t}\big[10A\,t + (7A+10B)\big]. $$

Match $t e^{t}$: $10A=1\Rightarrow A=\tfrac{1}{10}$; $7A+10B=0\Rightarrow B=-\tfrac{7}{100}$.

Answer (matches key): $\quad y = c_1 e^{-4t} + c_2 e^{-t} + \dfrac{1}{10}t\,e^{t} - \dfrac{7}{100}e^{t}.$

2d)   $y'' + y = \tan t,\quad -\tfrac{\pi}{2}\lt t\lt\tfrac{\pi}{2}$

Homogeneous. $r=\pm i$, so $y_1=\cos t,\ y_2=\sin t$, and $y_h=c_1\cos t+c_2\sin t$.

Wronskian. $W = \cos t\cos t - (-\sin t)\sin t = \cos^2 t+\sin^2 t = 1$.

The equation is already in standard form, so $g=\tan t$. Then

$$ u_1' = -\frac{y_2 g}{W} = -\sin t\tan t = -\frac{\sin^2 t}{\cos t}, \qquad u_2' = \frac{y_1 g}{W} = \cos t\tan t = \sin t. $$

Integrate. Easily $u_2 = -\cos t$. For $u_1$, write $\dfrac{\sin^2 t}{\cos t}=\dfrac{1-\cos^2 t}{\cos t}=\sec t-\cos t$:

$$ u_1 = -\!\int(\sec t-\cos t)\,dt = -\ln|\sec t+\tan t| + \sin t. $$
the awkward $\dfrac{\sin^2 t}{\cos t}$ can't be integrated as-is, so we trade $\sin^2 t$ for $1-\cos^2 t$ (the Pythagorean identity). That splits it into $\sec t - \cos t$ ŌĆö two pieces we do have on the integral sheet.

Assemble $y_p=u_1 y_1+u_2 y_2$:

$$ y_p = \big(-\ln|\sec t+\tan t|+\sin t\big)\cos t + (-\cos t)\sin t = -\cos t\,\ln|\sec t+\tan t|, $$

since the two $\sin t\cos t$ terms cancel. Finally $\sec t+\tan t=\dfrac{1+\sin t}{\cos t}$.

Answer (matches key): $\quad y = c_1\cos t + c_2\sin t - \cos t\,\ln\!\left(\dfrac{1+\sin t}{\cos t}\right).$

2e)   $y'' - 2y' + y = \dfrac{e^{t}}{t},\quad t\gt 0$

Homogeneous. $(r-1)^2=0\Rightarrow r=1$ double, so $y_1=e^{t},\ y_2=t e^{t}$.

Wronskian. With $y_2'=e^{t}(1+t)$,

$$ W = e^{t}\cdot e^{t}(1+t) - e^{t}\cdot t e^{t} = e^{2t}(1+t) - t e^{2t} = e^{2t}. $$

Standard form already, $g=e^{t}/t$. Then

$$ u_1' = -\frac{(t e^{t})(e^{t}/t)}{e^{2t}} = -\frac{e^{2t}}{e^{2t}} = -1, \qquad u_2' = \frac{(e^{t})(e^{t}/t)}{e^{2t}} = \frac{1}{t}. $$ $$ u_1 = -t, \qquad u_2 = \ln t. $$

Assemble.

$$ y_p = (-t)e^{t} + (\ln t)\,t e^{t} = t e^{t}\ln t - t e^{t}. $$

The $-t e^{t}$ term is just $-1\cdot y_2$, a homogeneous solution, so it is absorbed into $c_2$.

whenever a piece of $y_p$ is already one of the "natural" solutions, it's pointless to keep it separate ŌĆö it just shifts the value of the free constant $c_2$. So we quietly drop it and let $c_2$ swallow it.
Answer (matches key): $\quad y = c_1 e^{t} + c_2 t e^{t} + t e^{t}\ln t.$

2f)   $y'' + y' - 6y = \dfrac{1}{e^{t}+1},\quad t\gt 0$

Homogeneous. $r^2+r-6=(r+3)(r-2)=0\Rightarrow r=-3,2$, so $y_1=e^{-3t},\ y_2=e^{2t}$.

Wronskian. $W = e^{-3t}(2e^{2t}) - (-3e^{-3t})(e^{2t}) = 2e^{-t}+3e^{-t} = 5e^{-t}.$

With $g=\dfrac{1}{e^{t}+1}$,

$$ u_1' = -\frac{e^{2t}}{5e^{-t}}\cdot\frac{1}{e^{t}+1} = -\frac{e^{3t}}{5(e^{t}+1)}, \qquad u_2' = \frac{e^{-3t}}{5e^{-t}}\cdot\frac{1}{e^{t}+1} = \frac{e^{-2t}}{5(e^{t}+1)}. $$

Substitute $w=e^{t}$ (so $dw=e^{t}\,dt$, i.e. $dt=\dfrac{dw}{w}$). For $u_1$:

$$ u_1 = -\frac15\int\frac{w^3}{w+1}\frac{dw}{w} = -\frac15\int\frac{w^2}{w+1}\,dw = -\frac15\int\Big(w-1+\frac{1}{w+1}\Big)dw = -\frac15\Big(\frac{w^2}{2}-w+\ln(w+1)\Big). $$
the substitution $w=e^t$ turns the scary exponential fraction into an ordinary algebra fraction in $w$. Then $\dfrac{w^2}{w+1}$ is "top-heavy" (numerator degree $\ge$ denominator), so we do polynomial division to get $w-1+\dfrac{1}{w+1}$ ŌĆö three things we can integrate one at a time.

Back-substitute $w=e^t$: $\;u_1 = -\dfrac{e^{2t}}{10}+\dfrac{e^{t}}{5}-\dfrac{1}{5}\ln(e^{t}+1).$

For $u_2$, the integrand becomes $\dfrac{1}{5\,w^3(w+1)}$. Partial fractions (the decomposition is on the official sheet):

$$ \frac{1}{w^3(w+1)} = \frac{1}{w}-\frac{1}{w^2}+\frac{1}{w^3}-\frac{1}{w+1}, $$ $$ u_2 = \frac15\Big(\ln w + \frac1w - \frac{1}{2w^2} - \ln(w+1)\Big) = \frac{t}{5}+\frac{e^{-t}}{5}-\frac{e^{-2t}}{10}-\frac15\ln(e^{t}+1). $$

Assemble $y_p=u_1 e^{-3t}+u_2 e^{2t}$ and group by $\tfrac15$:

$$ y_p = \frac15\Big( t e^{2t} + e^{t} - \tfrac12 - \tfrac12 e^{-t} + e^{-2t} - \big(e^{2t}+e^{-3t}\big)\ln(e^{t}+1)\Big). $$
Answer (matches key): $ \displaystyle y = c_1 e^{-3t} + c_2 e^{2t} + \frac15\Big( t e^{2t} + e^{t} - \tfrac12 - \tfrac12 e^{-t} + e^{-2t} - \big(e^{2t}+e^{-3t}\big)\ln(e^{t}+1)\Big).$

This monster was confirmed two ways: by integrating $u_1,u_2$ as above, and by plugging the whole $y_p$ back into the ODE ŌĆö both give exactly $\dfrac{1}{e^t+1}$.
