Hessian, Jacobian & the Chain Rule

Classifying critical points of a scalar function with the Hessian, finding and judging the stability of equilibria of a vector field with the Jacobian, and using the multivariable chain rule (including implicit differentiation).

Start here — the whole idea in plain words

Keep the landscape picture from Worksheet 8. This sheet is about two questions: where are the peaks and valleys? and if a system rolls around on its own, where does it settle and does it stay?

Critical point = a flat spot. Where the ground is perfectly level ($\nabla f=0$). But "flat" could mean a valley bottom, a hilltop, or a mountain pass.

Hessian = the curvature check that tells which one. At a flat spot, look at how the ground curves:

Picture this: curves up in every direction → you're in a bowl → minimum. Curves down in every direction → you're on a dome → maximum. Curves up one way but down another → a Pringle chip / saddle. The "eigenvalues" of the Hessian are just the curvatures along its main directions: all positive = bowl, all negative = dome, mixed signs = saddle.

Jacobian = the local "flow" behaviour. Imagine arrows drawn at every point showing which way things drift (like wind on a weather map). The Jacobian is the little matrix describing how that flow stretches and twists right next to a point.

Stability = does a small nudge get pulled back or flung away?

Picture this: a marble at the bottom of a bowl rolls back when nudged — stable. A marble balanced on top of a dome rolls off — unstable. The eigenvalues of the Jacobian are the verdict: negative real parts pull back (stable), any positive real part flings away (unstable).

Chain rule = combining "how steep" with "how you're moving." If you walk a trail across the hill, your altitude changes at a rate of (steepness of hill) combined with (your velocity) — that's the dot product $\nabla V\cdot x'(t)$. In 3a this quantity turns out to never be positive, meaning a certain "energy" $V$ only ever drains away.

Implicit differentiation (3b): when $x$ and $t$ are tangled together in one equation and you can't cleanly solve for $x$, you can still differentiate the whole equation as it stands — just remember $x$ secretly depends on $t$.

The tools

Critical points and the Hessian (second-derivative test)

For a scalar function $f(x_1,\dots,x_n)$, a critical point is where the gradient vanishes, $\nabla f(x_0)=0$. To classify it, use the Hessian, the symmetric matrix of second partials:

$$ H_f(x) = \left[\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_i\,\partial x_j}\right]_{i,j}. $$

Evaluate $H_f$ at the critical point and look at its eigenvalues:

Eigenvalues of $H_f(x_0)$$H_f(x_0)$ is$x_0$ is a
all $\gt 0$positive definitelocal minimum
all $\lt 0$negative definitelocal maximum
both signs presentindefinitesaddle point

(If some eigenvalue is $0$ the test is inconclusive.) This is the $n$-dimensional version of the single-variable test "$f''\gt0\Rightarrow$ min, $f''\lt0\Rightarrow$ max."

Jacobian and linear stability

For a vector field $f:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^n$, $f=(f_1,\dots,f_n)$, the Jacobian is the matrix of all first partials:

$$ J_f(x) = \left[\frac{\partial f_i}{\partial x_j}\right]_{i,j}. $$

A critical point (equilibrium) of the system $x'=f(x)$ is a point with $f(x_0)=0$. The worksheet gives the stability rule directly via the eigenvalues $\lambda$ of $J_f(x_0)$:

Eigenvalue conditionVerdict
$\operatorname{Re}(\lambda)\lt0$ for all eigenvaluesasymptotically stable
$\operatorname{Re}(\lambda)\gt0$ for some eigenvaluenot stable

For a $2\times2$ matrix, eigenvalues solve $\lambda^2-(\operatorname{tr})\lambda+\det=0$, i.e. $\lambda=\dfrac{\operatorname{tr}\pm\sqrt{\operatorname{tr}^2-4\det}}{2}$.

Multivariable chain rule & implicit differentiation

If $x(t)=(x_1(t),\dots,x_n(t))$ is a curve and $V$ a scalar function, then

$$ \frac{d}{dt}V(x(t)) = \nabla V(x(t))\cdot x'(t) = \sum_i \frac{\partial V}{\partial x_i}\,x_i'(t). $$

For implicit relations $F(t,x(t))=c$, just differentiate both sides in $t$, applying the product/chain rules and remembering $\dfrac{d}{dt}x(t)=x'(t)$.

Exercise 1 — Local extrema via the Hessian

$f(x_1,x_2,x_3)=x_1x_2x_3+\dfrac{1}{x_1}+\dfrac{1}{x_2}+\dfrac{1}{x_3}$ on $(\mathbb{R}\setminus\{0\})^3$.

Step 1 — find the critical points

Set each partial to zero (using $\dfrac{d}{dx}\tfrac1x=-\tfrac1{x^2}$):

$$ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}=x_2x_3-\frac{1}{x_1^2}=0,\quad \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2}=x_1x_3-\frac{1}{x_2^2}=0,\quad \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_3}=x_1x_2-\frac{1}{x_3^2}=0. $$

Trick: multiply the first equation by $x_1$, the second by $x_2$, the third by $x_3$:

$$ x_1x_2x_3=\frac{1}{x_1},\qquad x_1x_2x_3=\frac{1}{x_2},\qquad x_1x_2x_3=\frac{1}{x_3}. $$
multiplying each equation by its own variable is a trick to make the three equations look the same on the left ($x_1x_2x_3$ every time). Once the left sides match, the right sides must match too, which instantly forces $x_1=x_2=x_3$ — turning a 3-variable mess into one easy equation.

The right-hand sides are all equal, so $\dfrac1{x_1}=\dfrac1{x_2}=\dfrac1{x_3}$, hence $x_1=x_2=x_3=:a$. Then $x_1x_2x_3=a^3$ must equal $\dfrac1a$, giving $a^4=1$, so (for real $a\neq0$) $a=\pm1$.

Critical points: $\;(1,1,1)\;$ and $\;(-1,-1,-1)$.

Step 2 — the Hessian

The second partials are $\dfrac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_i^2}=\dfrac{2}{x_i^3}$ and $\dfrac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1\partial x_2}=x_3$, $\dfrac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1\partial x_3}=x_2$, $\dfrac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_2\partial x_3}=x_1$, so

$$ H_f=\begin{pmatrix} 2/x_1^3 & x_3 & x_2\\ x_3 & 2/x_2^3 & x_1\\ x_2 & x_1 & 2/x_3^3 \end{pmatrix}. $$

Step 3 — classify each point

At $(1,1,1)$:

$$ H_f(1,1,1)=\begin{pmatrix}2&1&1\\1&2&1\\1&1&2\end{pmatrix}=I+\mathbf{1}, $$

where $\mathbf 1$ is the all-ones matrix. Since $\mathbf 1$ has eigenvalues $3$ (eigenvector $(1,1,1)$) and $0$ (double), $H_f=I+\mathbf 1$ has eigenvalues $1+3=4$ and $1+0=1$ (double): $\{4,1,1\}$. All positive $\Rightarrow$ positive definite $\Rightarrow$ local minimum, with value $f(1,1,1)=1+1+1+1=4$.

At $(-1,-1,-1)$: every diagonal entry is $2/(-1)^3=-2$ and every off-diagonal is $-1$, so

$$ H_f(-1,-1,-1)=-\begin{pmatrix}2&1&1\\1&2&1\\1&1&2\end{pmatrix}, $$

with eigenvalues $\{-4,-1,-1\}$. All negative $\Rightarrow$ negative definite $\Rightarrow$ local maximum, with value $f(-1,-1,-1)=(-1)+(-1)+(-1)+(-1)=-4$.

Result: local minimum at $(1,1,1)$ with $f=4$; local maximum at $(-1,-1,-1)$ with $f=-4$.

Exercise 2 — Jacobian, critical points & stability

$f(x)=\big(3x_1x_2-2x_2^2+11,\ \ x_1+3x_2\big)$.

(a) The Jacobian

With $f_1=3x_1x_2-2x_2^2+11$ and $f_2=x_1+3x_2$, compute the four partials one at a time (treat the other variable as a constant):

$$ \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_1}=3x_2,\qquad \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_2}=3x_1-4x_2,\qquad \frac{\partial f_2}{\partial x_1}=1,\qquad \frac{\partial f_2}{\partial x_2}=3. $$

Stack them so that row $i$ holds the derivatives of $f_i$:

$$ J_f(x)=\begin{pmatrix}\partial f_1/\partial x_1 & \partial f_1/\partial x_2\\[2pt] \partial f_2/\partial x_1 & \partial f_2/\partial x_2\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}3x_2 & 3x_1-4x_2\\[2pt] 1 & 3\end{pmatrix}. $$

(b) Critical points: $f(x_0)=0$

From $f_2=x_1+3x_2=0$ we get $x_1=-3x_2$. Substitute into $f_1=0$:

$$ 3(-3x_2)x_2-2x_2^2+11=-9x_2^2-2x_2^2+11=-11x_2^2+11=0 \;\Rightarrow\; x_2^2=1\;\Rightarrow\; x_2=\pm1. $$

So $x_2=1\Rightarrow x_1=-3$ and $x_2=-1\Rightarrow x_1=3$.

Critical points: $\;(-3,1)\;$ and $\;(3,-1)$.

(c) Stability via eigenvalues of $J_f$

At $(-3,1)$:

$$ J_f(-3,1)=\begin{pmatrix}3 & 3(-3)-4(1)\\ 1 & 3\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}3 & -13\\ 1 & 3\end{pmatrix},\quad \operatorname{tr}=6,\ \det=9+13=22. $$ $$ \lambda=\frac{6\pm\sqrt{36-88}}{2}=\frac{6\pm\sqrt{-52}}{2}=3\pm i\sqrt{13}. $$

The real part is $\operatorname{Re}(\lambda)=3\gt0$ $\Rightarrow$ not stable (a spiral source).

two shortcuts at work. (1) For a 2×2 matrix you never need the full characteristic polynomial — just trace and determinant, since $\lambda=\tfrac{\operatorname{tr}\pm\sqrt{\operatorname{tr}^2-4\det}}{2}$. (2) The real part of $\lambda$ is the "pull-in vs fling-away" rate: positive means nearby points get pushed away over time, so the equilibrium can't be stable. The imaginary part just means the motion also spirals.

At $(3,-1)$:

$$ J_f(3,-1)=\begin{pmatrix}-3 & 3(3)-4(-1)\\ 1 & 3\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}-3 & 13\\ 1 & 3\end{pmatrix},\quad \operatorname{tr}=0,\ \det=-9-13=-22. $$ $$ \lambda=\frac{0\pm\sqrt{0-4(-22)}}{2}=\frac{\pm\sqrt{88}}{2}=\pm\sqrt{22}. $$

One eigenvalue $+\sqrt{22}\gt0$ $\Rightarrow$ not stable (a saddle).

Conclusion: both critical points are not stable — $(-3,1)$ because the complex eigenvalues have positive real part, $(3,-1)$ because it has a positive real eigenvalue.

Exercise 3 — Chain rule

(a) Show $\dfrac{d}{dt}V(x(t))\le0$

For $V(x)=\ln(1+x_1^2)+x_2^2$, the gradient is

$$ \nabla V=\Big(\frac{2x_1}{1+x_1^2},\ 2x_2\Big). $$

By the chain rule, with the given $x_1'=x_1(x_2-1)$ and $x_2'=-\dfrac{x_1^2}{1+x_1^2}$,

$$ \frac{d}{dt}V(x(t))=\frac{2x_1}{1+x_1^2}\,x_1' + 2x_2\,x_2' = \frac{2x_1}{1+x_1^2}\,x_1(x_2-1) + 2x_2\!\left(-\frac{x_1^2}{1+x_1^2}\right). $$

Put over the common denominator $1+x_1^2$:

$$ = \frac{2x_1^2(x_2-1) - 2x_1^2 x_2}{1+x_1^2} = \frac{2x_1^2 x_2 - 2x_1^2 - 2x_1^2 x_2}{1+x_1^2} = \frac{-2x_1^2}{1+x_1^2}. $$

Since $x_1^2\ge0$ and $1+x_1^2\gt0$, this is $\le0$ for all $t$.

to show a fraction is never positive, just check its sign-parts: the top $-2x_1^2$ is always $\le0$ (a square, negated), the bottom $1+x_1^2$ is always $\gt0$. Negative-or-zero divided by positive is negative-or-zero. Done — no calculus needed for the last step.
Shown: $\;\dfrac{d}{dt}V(x(t))=-\dfrac{2x_1(t)^2}{1+x_1(t)^2}\le0.$ (So $V$ is a Lyapunov function — it never increases along trajectories.)

(b) Implicit differentiation

Differentiate $x(t)^2+2t\,x(t)-t^2=c$ with respect to $t$. Term by term, using the product rule on $2t\,x(t)$ and the chain rule on $x(t)^2$:

$$ \frac{d}{dt}\big[x^2\big]=2x\,x',\qquad \frac{d}{dt}\big[2tx\big]=2x+2t\,x',\qquad \frac{d}{dt}\big[t^2\big]=2t,\qquad \frac{d}{dt}[c]=0. $$

Adding up:

$$ 2x\,x' + 2x + 2t\,x' - 2t = 0. $$

Group the $x'$ terms and the non-$x'$ terms:

$$ \underbrace{2x - 2t}_{=\,2(x-t)} \; + \; \underbrace{(2x+2t)}_{=\,2(x+t)}\,x' = 0. $$
Shown: $\;2\big(x(t)-t\big)+2\big(x(t)+t\big)x'(t)=0,$ exactly as required.
