Partial Derivatives, Gradient & Differentiability

The start of multivariable calculus: differentiating functions of several variables one variable at a time, the gradient and directional derivatives, tangent planes, and the subtle question of when a function is actually differentiable.

Core ideas

Partial derivatives

For $f(x_1,\dots,x_n)$, the partial derivative $\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}$ is the ordinary derivative with respect to $x_i$ while treating every other variable as a constant. Formally,

$$ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}(x) = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(x_1,\dots,x_i+h,\dots,x_n)-f(x_1,\dots,x_n)}{h}. $$

All the one-variable rules (product, quotient, chain) apply as usual ŌĆö you just hold the other letters fixed.

Differentiating an integral with a variable limit (FTC)

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus gives, for a variable upper/lower limit,

$$ \frac{d}{dx}\int_{a}^{x} h(t)\,dt = h(x), \qquad \frac{d}{dx}\int_{x}^{b} h(t)\,dt = -\,h(x). $$

This handles the "integral" functions in 1a(iii) and 1b(ii) without ever computing the integral.

Gradient and directional derivative

The gradient stacks all first partials into a vector:

$$ \nabla f(x) = \Big(\tfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_1},\,\tfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_2},\,\dots,\,\tfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}\Big). $$

The directional derivative of $f$ along a vector $v$ measures the rate of change of $f$ as you move in the direction $v$. When $f$ is differentiable it equals a dot product:

$$ \frac{\partial f}{\partial v}(x_0) \;=\; \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(x_0+hv)-f(x_0)}{h} \;=\; \nabla f(x_0)\cdot v. $$

Convention warning. Some books require $v$ to be a unit vector ($\lVert v\rVert=1$). This course uses the version above with the vector $v$ as given (not normalized) ŌĆö that is exactly what produces the answer in 2(ii). If a problem instead asks for the rate "per unit length," you would divide by $\lVert v\rVert$.

Tangent plane (linear approximation)

If $f(x_1,x_2)$ is differentiable at $x_0=(x_{01},x_{02})$, the graph $z=f(x_1,x_2)$ has the tangent plane

$$ z = f(x_0) + \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}(x_0)\,(x_1-x_{01}) + \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2}(x_0)\,(x_2-x_{02}). $$ Differentiability ŌĆö the careful definition

$f$ is differentiable at $x_0$ if it is well-approximated by a linear map there: there is a vector (the gradient) with

$$ \lim_{x\to x_0}\frac{\big|\,f(x)-f(x_0)-\nabla f(x_0)\cdot(x-x_0)\,\big|}{\lVert x-x_0\rVert} = 0. $$

The crucial logical chain (and its traps):

True implicationCommon trap (false!)
differentiable $\Rightarrow$ continuouspartials exist $\not\Rightarrow$ continuous
differentiable $\Rightarrow$ both partials existpartials exist $\not\Rightarrow$ differentiable
partials exist and are continuous near $x_0$ $\Rightarrow$ differentiablecontinuous $\not\Rightarrow$ differentiable

So to disprove differentiability, the quickest route is often to show $f$ is not even continuous (Exercise 3b), or that the limit above fails along some path (3c).

Exercise 1 ŌĆö Partial derivatives

1a ŌĆö at a specific point

(i)   $f(x_1,x_2)=\sqrt{3x_1+x_2^2}$ at $(1,2)$

Using $\dfrac{d}{du}\sqrt{u}=\dfrac{1}{2\sqrt u}$ together with the chain rule:

$$ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}=\frac{3}{2\sqrt{3x_1+x_2^2}},\qquad \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2}=\frac{2x_2}{2\sqrt{3x_1+x_2^2}}=\frac{x_2}{\sqrt{3x_1+x_2^2}}. $$

At $(1,2)$, $3x_1+x_2^2 = 3+4 = 7$:

$\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}(1,2)=\dfrac{3}{2\sqrt7},\quad \dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_2}(1,2)=\dfrac{2}{\sqrt7}$ ŌĆö matches the given answer.

(ii)   $f(x_1,x_2,x_3)=x_2\,e^{x_1+x_2x_3}$ at $(0,1,-1)$

Let $E=e^{x_1+x_2x_3}$. Differentiating (product rule where $x_2$ appears as a factor):

$$ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}=x_2 E,\qquad \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2}=E + x_2 E\,x_3 = E(1+x_2x_3),\qquad \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_3}=x_2 E\,x_2 = x_2^2 E. $$

At $(0,1,-1)$ the exponent is $0+(1)(-1)=-1$, so $E=e^{-1}$:

$\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}=\dfrac1e,\quad \dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_2}=e^{-1}(1-1)=0,\quad \dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_3}=\dfrac1e$ ŌĆö matches.

(iii)   $f(x_1,x_2)=8x_1^2+\displaystyle\int_{1}^{x_2}e^{-t^2}\,dt$ at $(3,1)$

The integral does not depend on $x_1$, so $\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}=16x_1$. For $x_2$, the integral has variable upper limit, so by the FTC $\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_2}=e^{-x_2^2}$ (we never need the value of the integral itself).

At $(3,1)$:

$\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}(3,1)=48,\quad \dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_2}(3,1)=e^{-1}=\dfrac1e$ ŌĆö matches.

1b ŌĆö general formulas

(i)   $f(x_1,x_2)=\ln\!\big(x_1+\sqrt{x_1^2+x_2^2}\big)$

Write $R=\sqrt{x_1^2+x_2^2}$ and use $\dfrac{d}{du}\ln u=\dfrac{u'}{u}$. For $x_1$, the inside derivative is $1+\dfrac{x_1}{R}=\dfrac{R+x_1}{R}$, so

$$ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}=\frac{1}{x_1+R}\cdot\frac{R+x_1}{R}=\frac{1}{R}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{x_1^2+x_2^2}}. $$

For $x_2$, the inside derivative is $\dfrac{x_2}{R}$, so

$$ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2}=\frac{1}{x_1+R}\cdot\frac{x_2}{R}=\frac{x_2}{\big(x_1+\sqrt{x_1^2+x_2^2}\big)\sqrt{x_1^2+x_2^2}}. $$
Both match the given answer. (Nice surprise: the $x_1$-partial collapses to $1/R$.)

(ii)   $f(x_1,x_2)=\displaystyle\int_{x_1}^{x_2}\cos(t^2)\,dt$

Variable lower limit $x_1$ gives a minus sign; variable upper limit $x_2$ gives a plus sign (FTC):

$\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}=-\cos(x_1^2),\qquad \dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_2}=\cos(x_2^2)$ ŌĆö matches.

(iii)   $f(x_1,x_2,x_3,x_4)=\dfrac{x_1-x_2}{x_3-x_4}$

Treat $\dfrac{1}{x_3-x_4}$ as a constant for the top variables, and use the power/chain rule $(x_3-x_4)^{-1}$ for the bottom ones:

$$ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}=\frac{1}{x_3-x_4},\quad \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2}=\frac{-1}{x_3-x_4}=\frac{1}{x_4-x_3}, $$ $$ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_3}=(x_1-x_2)\cdot\frac{-1}{(x_3-x_4)^2}=\frac{x_2-x_1}{(x_3-x_4)^2},\quad \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_4}=(x_1-x_2)\cdot\frac{-1}{(x_3-x_4)^2}\cdot(-1)=\frac{x_1-x_2}{(x_3-x_4)^2}. $$
All four match the given answer.

Exercise 2 ŌĆö Gradient & directional derivative

(a)   Gradient of $f(x_1,x_2)=\arctan\!\Big(\dfrac{x_1+x_2}{x_1-x_2}\Big)$

Let $u=\dfrac{x_1+x_2}{x_1-x_2}$. Then $\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}=\dfrac{1}{1+u^2}\,\dfrac{\partial u}{\partial x_i}$ since $\dfrac{d}{du}\arctan u=\dfrac{1}{1+u^2}$.

Inside derivatives (quotient rule):

$$ \frac{\partial u}{\partial x_1}=\frac{(x_1-x_2)-(x_1+x_2)}{(x_1-x_2)^2}=\frac{-2x_2}{(x_1-x_2)^2},\qquad \frac{\partial u}{\partial x_2}=\frac{(x_1-x_2)+(x_1+x_2)}{(x_1-x_2)^2}=\frac{2x_1}{(x_1-x_2)^2}. $$

The $1+u^2$ factor simplifies beautifully:

$$ 1+u^2=\frac{(x_1-x_2)^2+(x_1+x_2)^2}{(x_1-x_2)^2}=\frac{2(x_1^2+x_2^2)}{(x_1-x_2)^2}. $$

Multiplying, the $(x_1-x_2)^2$ cancel:

$$ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}=\frac{(x_1-x_2)^2}{2(x_1^2+x_2^2)}\cdot\frac{-2x_2}{(x_1-x_2)^2}=\frac{-x_2}{x_1^2+x_2^2},\qquad \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2}=\frac{x_1}{x_1^2+x_2^2}. $$
$\nabla f=\Big(-\dfrac{x_2}{x_1^2+x_2^2},\ \dfrac{x_1}{x_1^2+x_2^2}\Big)$ ŌĆö matches.

(ii)   Directional derivative of $f(x)=\dfrac{1}{x_1+2x_2-3x_3}$ at $x_0=(1,1,-1)$ along $v=(12,-9,-4)$

Let $D=x_1+2x_2-3x_3$, so $f=D^{-1}$ and $\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}=-D^{-2}\dfrac{\partial D}{\partial x_i}$ with $\partial D=(1,2,-3)$.

At $x_0$: $D=1+2(1)-3(-1)=1+2+3=6$, so $D^2=36$ and

$$ \nabla f(x_0)=\Big(-\tfrac{1}{36},\,-\tfrac{2}{36},\,\tfrac{3}{36}\Big)=\Big(-\tfrac{1}{36},\,-\tfrac{1}{18},\,\tfrac{1}{12}\Big). $$

Dot with the given vector $v=(12,-9,-4)$:

$$ \frac{\partial f}{\partial v}(x_0)=\nabla f(x_0)\cdot v = -\tfrac{12}{36}+\tfrac{(-9)(-1)}{18}+\tfrac{-4}{12} = -\tfrac13+\tfrac12-\tfrac13 = -\tfrac16. $$
$\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial v}(1,1,-1)=-\dfrac16$ ŌĆö matches. (This uses $v$ directly, confirming the non-normalized convention.)

Exercise 3 ŌĆö Differentiability

(a)   Tangent plane to $f(x_1,x_2)=e^{x_2^2-x_1^2}$ at $x_0=(-1,1)$

Value. $f(-1,1)=e^{1-1}=e^0=1$.

Partials. With $E=e^{x_2^2-x_1^2}$,

$$ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}=-2x_1 E,\qquad \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2}=2x_2 E. $$

At $(-1,1)$, $E=1$, so $\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}=-2(-1)=2$ and $\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_2}=2(1)=2$.

Plane.

$$ z = 1 + 2\,(x_1-(-1)) + 2\,(x_2-1) = 1 + 2(x_1+1) + 2(x_2-1). $$
Tangent plane: $\;z = 2x_1 + 2x_2 + 1.$

(b)   $f(x_1,x_2)=\dfrac{x_1x_2}{x_1^2+x_2^2}$ (and $f(0,0)=0$): partials at the origin & differentiability

Partial $\partial f/\partial x_1(0,0)$. Approach along the $x_1$-axis ($x_2=0$): $f(h,0)=\dfrac{h\cdot 0}{h^2}=0$, so

$$ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}(0,0)=\lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(h,0)-f(0,0)}{h}=\lim_{h\to0}\frac{0-0}{h}=0. $$

By the same computation with $x_1=0$, $\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_2}(0,0)=0$. So both partials exist and equal $0$.

Differentiable? First test continuity at the origin (a necessary condition). Approach along the line $x_2=x_1$, i.e. $x_1=x_2=s\to0$:

$$ f(s,s)=\frac{s\cdot s}{s^2+s^2}=\frac{s^2}{2s^2}=\frac12 \;\not\to\; 0=f(0,0). $$

Along the axis $x_2=0$ instead, $f\equiv 0$. Two paths give two different limits ($\tfrac12$ and $0$), so $\displaystyle\lim_{(x_1,x_2)\to(0,0)}f$ does not exist ŌĆö $f$ is not continuous at $(0,0)$.

Conclusion: $\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}(0,0)=\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_2}(0,0)=0$, but $f$ is not differentiable at $(0,0)$ (it isn't even continuous there). A textbook example that "partials exist" is far weaker than "differentiable."

(c)   $f(x_1,x_2)=|x_2|^{\alpha}\sin(x_1)$ (and $f=0$ when $x_2=0$): for which $\alpha$ is $f$ (i) continuous, (ii) differentiable at $(0,0)$?

Note $f(0,0)=0$, and both partials at the origin will turn out to be $0$ (computed below).

(i) Continuity at $(0,0)$

We use the bound $|\sin x_1|\le|x_1|$ and the fact that $|x_2|\le\lVert(x_1,x_2)\rVert$ and $|x_1|\le\lVert(x_1,x_2)\rVert$, writing $\rho=\lVert(x_1,x_2)\rVert$.

Case $\alpha\ge0$ (continuous). For $\alpha>0$,

$$ |f| = |x_2|^{\alpha}\,|\sin x_1| \le \rho^{\alpha}\cdot|x_1| \le \rho^{\alpha}\cdot\rho = \rho^{\alpha+1}\to0. $$

For $\alpha=0$, simply $|f|=|\sin x_1|\le|x_1|\to0$. Either way the limit is $0=f(0,0)$, so $f$ is continuous.

Case $\alpha\lt0$ (not continuous). Take the path $x_2=s,\ x_1=s^{2}$ with $s\to0^+$ (it does tend to the origin). Then

$$ f = s^{\alpha}\sin(s^{2}) \sim s^{\alpha}\cdot s^{2} = s^{\alpha+2}. $$

More directly, choose $x_1=s,\ x_2=s^{2}$: $\,f=(s^{2})^{\alpha}\sin s = s^{2\alpha}\sin s\sim s^{2\alpha+1}$. For $\alpha\le-\tfrac12$ this blows up or stays nonzero (e.g. $\alpha=-\tfrac12$ gives $\to1$), and for any $\alpha\lt0$ a slightly steeper path $x_1=s^{p}$ with $0\lt p\lt|\alpha|$ gives $s^{\alpha+p}\to\infty$. So the limit is not $0$, and $f$ is not continuous.

(i) Continuous at $(0,0)\iff \alpha\ge0$.

(ii) Differentiability at $(0,0)$

Partials at the origin. On the $x_1$-axis $f(h,0)=0$ (the $x_2=0$ branch), so $\partial f/\partial x_1(0,0)=0$. On the $x_2$-axis $f(0,h)=|h|^{\alpha}\sin 0 = 0$, so $\partial f/\partial x_2(0,0)=0$. Hence the candidate gradient is $0$, and the differentiability limit to check is

$$ \lim_{(x_1,x_2)\to(0,0)}\frac{|f(x_1,x_2)|}{\rho}=\lim\frac{|x_2|^{\alpha}|\sin x_1|}{\sqrt{x_1^2+x_2^2}}. $$

Case $\alpha\gt0$ (differentiable). Since $|\sin x_1|\le|x_1|\le\rho$,

$$ \frac{|x_2|^{\alpha}|\sin x_1|}{\rho}\le |x_2|^{\alpha}\cdot\frac{|x_1|}{\rho}\le|x_2|^{\alpha}\to0. $$

Case $\alpha=0$ (not differentiable). The ratio is $\dfrac{|\sin x_1|}{\rho}$. Along $x_2=0,\ x_1=s$: $\dfrac{|\sin s|}{|s|}\to1\neq0$. The limit fails.

Case $\alpha\lt0$: not even continuous, so not differentiable.

(ii) Differentiable at $(0,0)\iff \alpha\gt0$.

Neat takeaway: $\alpha\ge0$ buys continuity, but you need the strict $\alpha\gt0$ for differentiability ŌĆö the borderline $\alpha=0$ is continuous yet not differentiable.
